Vedic & Astronomical Computations

1. Technical Architecture: Zero External Dependencies
This dashboard computes rigorous orbital mechanics purely client-side. It does not rely on static JSON lookups, third-party REST APIs (like VedicRishi or ProKerala), or heavy external ephemeris libraries (like Swiss Ephemeris). The JavaScript engine mathematically calculates the positions of the Sun and Moon based on the host device's local system clock and embedded geographical coordinates.
2. Surya Siddhanta vs. Drik Siddhanta
Historically, Panchangams were calculated using the Surya Siddhanta, an ancient text containing fixed mathematical constants. Because the Earth wobbles on its axis (Precession of the Equinoxes), ancient constants have drifted over millennia, resulting in observable errors.

Modern precision astrology relies on Drik Siddhanta (observational/computational accuracy), matching NASA JPL calculations. This dashboard utilizes algorithms published by astronomer Jean Meeus to calculate the True Longitude of celestial bodies, adhering strictly to Drik Siddhanta principles.
3. Calculating Sunrise & Local Noon precisely
To find exact local Sunrise and Sunset, the algorithm calculates the Julian Century T to derive the Sun's Mean Anomaly and applies the Equation of Center. By factoring in the Mean Obliquity of the Ecliptic, the Sun's declination is found.

The standard Hour Angle formula is cos(ω) = (sin(h₀) - sin(φ) * sin(δ)) / (cos(φ) * cos(δ)). For true observational Sunrise, the altitude h₀ is set to -0.833°. This accounts for both the Sun's apparent semi-diameter (0.266°) and atmospheric refraction (0.567°), which "bends" light over the horizon before the Sun mathematically clears it.
4. Astrological Time Segments (Muhurtas & Kaalams)
In Vedic timekeeping, daylight (Dinamana) is not a fixed 12 hours. It is the precise difference in milliseconds between local Sunrise and Sunset.

This Dinamana is divided into 8 equal parts (approx 1.5 hours each) to calculate Rahu Kaalam, Yamagandam, and Gulika Kaalam. Depending on the day of the week (Vaara), specific segments are assigned to these planetary nodes. For example, on a Sunday, Rahu Kaalam always occupies the 8th segment of daylight.

Similarly, the daylight is divided into 15 parts to find the Abijith Muhurth, which corresponds to the 8th segment (the exact middle of the day, centering over Local Solar Noon).
5. Lunar Mechanics: Tithi & Nakshatra
Tithi: A lunar day. A new Tithi begins the moment the longitudinal angle between the Moon and the Sun increases by exactly 12°. Mathematically: Floor((Moon_Longitude - Sun_Longitude) / 12) + 1.

Nakshatra: A lunar mansion. The 360° ecliptic is divided by 27, resulting in segments of 13°20' (13.333°). The current Nakshatra is determined by the Moon's exact True Longitude divided by 13.333°.

Note on precision: Due to the massive perturbation data required for sub-minute lunar accuracy (accounting for Venus/Jupiter gravity), this standalone script uses a truncated Meeus expansion. It provides a highly reliable approximation but enterprise-grade exact minute-resolutions for ritualistic purposes strictly require multi-megabyte C++ ephemeris files (like DE440).
